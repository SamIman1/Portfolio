UX Research · Master's Thesis · Breda University of Applied Sciences · 2025

The Unusual Suspects

Examining the Meaning of Gaming in the Lives of Older Players

Research Type

Qualitative — Exploratory

Method

Semi-structured interviews + online community observation

Participants

9 players aged 50+, over 10 weeks

Tools

MaxQDA 24 · Thematic analysis · Grounded theory

The Question

Older adults make up 11% of the AAA gaming demographic and over 23% of monthly PC players — and that share is growing as populations age. Yet the games industry has historically designed and marketed almost exclusively for younger audiences. Academic research on older players, where it exists at all, tends to focus narrowly on health benefits and casual mobile games.

This left a straightforward question largely unanswered: what do games actually mean to people who have been playing for decades? What keeps them engaged, and what does that tell us about how games could be designed and marketed differently?

Why Qualitative

A survey could tell us what older players do — genres they prefer, how often they play. It cannot tell us why those choices matter to them, or how gaming fits into the texture of their daily lives. I chose semi-structured interviews because the research question was exploratory: I needed to follow participants where they led, not constrain them to predefined categories.

Participants were recruited primarily through the Old Timers Guild — one of the largest online gaming communities specifically for 50+ players — using snowball sampling to reach a diverse range of play styles and backgrounds. Interviews were conducted over ten weeks via Discord, Zoom, and Teams, transcribed, anonymised, and analysed iteratively in MaxQDA using inductive coding grounded in grounded theory principles. Each round of analysis informed the direction of subsequent interviews.

Key Findings

1
Socialisation is the dominant source of meaning

Across nearly every participant, gaming was deeply embedded in their social lives — playing with friends, family members, or structured communities like the OTG. Games were described as a richer way to catch up with people than a phone call, a space where personality facets emerge that aren't visible in everyday life.

2
The "gamer" label is actively rejected

Only one of nine participants self-identified as a gamer. The label was associated with toxic online culture, competitive behaviour, and a political dimension most participants wanted no part of — yet these same participants played regularly and invested deeply in the medium.

3
Eudaimonic play over hedonic play

Older players described a clear shift away from competitive, high-stakes gameplay toward mastery, strategy, storytelling, and cooperation. They were not playing to win; they were playing to experience something meaningful — consistent with Socioemotional Selectivity Theory's prediction that older adults prioritise emotionally significant, present-oriented goals.

4
Games create a need for socialisation they don't fulfil

The most unexpected finding: games generate the desire for social connection but lack the infrastructure to support it. Players consistently relied on external tools — Discord, WhatsApp groups — to organise sessions and maintain community. The disappearance of arcades removed a physical social space that was never replaced in the digital era.

"The game gives us a forum to talk and have things to talk about, even if we're just talking about what we did during the week."

— Participant 7, on their weekly Tuesday gaming sessions

Implications for Design

If meaning for older players is primarily social and eudaimonic, then features like in-game lobby chat, community tooling, and cooperative play modes are not secondary — they are the product. The current reliance on player-run communities and external software to fill the socialisation gap is fragile, and creates unnecessary friction for a demographic already navigating a fast-moving technology landscape.

The study also surfaced a methodological insight: "meaning of games" is too narrow a frame. "Meaning of play" — encompassing the social rituals, routines, and relationships that surround the act of gaming — is more accurate, and more useful as a design brief.

"For people at my age or older who are homebound for whatever reason — games give them a horizon to seek. A social life, even if it's online."

— Participant 4

Limitations & What I'd Do Differently

The sample was recruited primarily through the Old Timers Guild, which skews toward socially motivated players — people who had already sought out a community. This may overrepresent socialisation as a source of meaning compared to solo players who never pursued structured communities. A follow-up study would deliberately recruit through solo-focused channels to test whether the social findings hold more broadly.

The study was conducted entirely online, which limited observational depth. Ethnographic methods — sitting alongside players during actual sessions — would add behavioural data that interview transcripts alone cannot capture.

Railway Empire 2

Strategy-management game set in the golden age of rail transportation

Genre

Strategy, Simulation

Duration

February – June 2023

Team Size

~30 people

Primary Role

QA · Game Design · Balancing

Scenario Design Balancing Quality Assurance

Scenario Design

Railway Empire 2 ships with 14 gameplay scenarios alongside its 5 main campaigns. Given my interest in systems design, I took ownership of two of them.

Sitting on the Fence

The more complex of the two scenarios. I added a strategic dimension to the classic gameplay loop: the player must choose a side in the Franco-Prussian War and use their railway network to support the war effort. Prussia's historical advantage — the speed and logistics of its rail network — is reflected in the scenario's design: some of the tightest objective deadlines in the game.

The French side introduces a counterbalancing challenge. France at the time was a highly centralised state, which I reflected through objectives that predominantly require supplying Paris — shifting the core challenge from speed to traffic optimisation.

Producing these scenarios required cross-discipline collaboration: I coordinated feedback requests with the narrative designer and several programmers, and maintained updated design documentation throughout to keep the team aligned.

Game Mechanics Balancing

Player Characters

Before starting a game, players choose a character that provides buffs and debuffs intended to encourage different play styles. By the time I arrived, most characters had functionally identical effects — their mechanical distinctions had been lost during production. Together with the narrative designer, I produced a redesign proposal for every character, consulting with programmers and artists to ensure our changes were feasible without significant new work. We presented the proposal to the studio lead and it shipped in the final game.

The changes addressed three specific problems: bonuses tied to cut mechanics, bonuses that failed to create meaningfully different play incentives, and characters whose mechanical identity didn't match their narrative archetype.

Special Wagons

During QA I discovered it was possible to break the game's economy through Special Wagons — a mechanic that trades locomotive capacity for bonuses. Using them was objectively correct in every situation, which removed meaningful choice.

The fix I proposed introduced a cost to Special Wagons, creating a genuine early-game decision between a safer mixed-line strategy and a riskier, potentially higher-return strategy with Special Wagons from the start.

Campaign Balancing

Once I had a strong grasp of the game's mechanics and design intent, I contributed to balancing later campaigns and scenarios that hadn't received many iterations. Most changes focused on reducing unintentional tedium caused by interactions between mechanics.

For example: in Campaign 1, I proposed removing business ownership from an AI rival, which made the "buy out your competitor" objective excessively tedious. In Campaign 2, I removed the AI's ability to sabotage the player — a drastic change justified by the fact that players had no effective countermeasures against three opponents over a five-hour campaign.

Quality Assurance

Over the course of the internship I worked within Gaming Minds Studios' QA pipeline, testing builds throughout development across PC, current-generation and previous-generation consoles. This gave me hands-on experience with professional QA tooling and the analytical skills required to identify and communicate bugs and their root causes.

Toward the end of the internship I took on an additional responsibility: compiling a structured FAQ document after the game's release by analysing recurring issues in post-launch player feedback. This was essentially a player research task — synthesising community feedback at scale into actionable information for the support team. The FAQ was published on Steam and is still live.

Child of Lothian

Third-person stealth set in 19th century Edinburgh

Genre

Third-person stealth

Duration

September 2021 – June 2022

Team Size

25 people

Primary Role

Technical Game Designer

Player Journal System

To encourage world exploration, the game uses setting-appropriate collectables with narrative text attached. Players find them scattered through the world; the journal is where they're displayed. I built the entire system from scratch in UE4 Blueprint.

The system is built around two blueprints — a Journal Widget and a Journal Entry. Journal entries pull images and narrative text from each collectable's data asset and translate them into the widget UI. The widget also handles collectable counting for achievements, and displays greyed-out entries for undiscovered items to give players a sense of what remains.

One learning from this system: I initially stored item data in Game Instance for easy access, which quickly became bloated as other designers stored their own data there too. In retrospect I'd use a dedicated save game object or data subsystem to keep concerns separated.

Level Design Tools

OpenLock Component

Early in the project I built a reusable component to give level designers a quick, configurable way to create key/lever door puzzles — connecting any activatable object as a "lever" and any item as a "key" that must be neutralised before a door opens.

As development progressed I extended the component with additional functionality requested by the team — including a sound event detectable by NPCs when a door is interacted with, supporting the stealth detection logic.

Hittable Signs

Since the sling is one of the player's primary ways of interacting with the world, I created a blueprint that allows environment objects to react naturally to projectile hits — activating physics on collision, then gracefully winding down after a timer.

The wind-down animation speed is exposed as a designer-adjustable variable, making it easy for environment artists to tune the feel without touching the Blueprint logic.

Technical Game Design · Audio Design · UE4 · BUas · 2020–2021

Osakabe

First-person horror set in a haunted Japanese castle

Genre

First-person horror

Duration

November 2020 – June 2021

Team Size

23 people

Roles

Technical Design · Audio Design

Player Camera & Movement

In a first-person horror, the player camera does more than frame the world — it communicates vulnerability, presence, and physicality. After researching comparable games I settled on two design goals: using subtle camera motion to build a stronger connection between player and avatar, and using camera height and angle to reinforce the feeling of being a small, vulnerable child in an oversized, hostile space.

Human walking is essentially a series of controlled falls, so I decided that footstep sounds — which feed directly into the NPC detection system — should be handled in the same Blueprint as the camera. I implemented camera position and rotation updates on timelines (one for idle, one for walking/running), with footstep sound events timed to coincide with camera movement for a cohesive sensory result.

Player Abilities

Wind-up Toy

A throwable toy the player can use to distract the enemy. We originally explored a possession mechanic that would let players control the toy and scout the environment from its perspective. In playtesting, this undercut one of our core pillars — we wanted the player to always feel small and vulnerable. Possession made the player feel safer and more powerful, so we cut it in favour of the toy as a simple throwable noisemaker that draws the enemy to its location.

Lantern

A limited-fuel light source that reveals items in darker areas while making the player more detectable to the monster — a direct risk/reward trade-off that reinforces the game's tense resource management.

Mirror

Allows the player to stun the monster briefly — but only if both the player and the monster are reflected simultaneously. We removed the ability to rotate the mirror after deciding not to model the player character, which neatly avoided the "player sees themselves" problem while still forcing the player to adopt a vulnerable facing position toward the monster.

Gameplay Prototype — Blind Navigation

Early in the project I worked in a strike team to prototype a blind navigation concept: a game character that perceives the world through senses other than sight. The brief limited us to a castle/fortress island setting.

My teammates built two core mechanics — fast-decaying echolocation and smell detection. I took ownership of the level: a labyrinth with a central riddle and several levers to find.

After testing, I iterated the layout to reduce confusion: adding distinct geometric landmarks in several rooms so players could build a reliable mental map even without visual information. The key insight was that spatial orientation in a blind navigation game depends entirely on memorable landmarks — something we had initially underestimated.

The Organisation

Breda Game City is a foundation dedicated to supporting and connecting the game development community in the Breda region — one of the most active game dev clusters in the Netherlands, anchored by Breda University of Applied Sciences and a growing number of independent studios. As coordinator, I'm the operational backbone of the foundation: I keep the programmes running, the stakeholders connected, and the community growing.

Breda Game Drinks

Breda Game Drinks is the foundation's flagship monthly networking event, bringing together game developers, students, studios, and industry partners in an informal setting. I took ownership of the full event cycle — from venue coordination and speaker outreach to hosting and post-event follow-up.

Industry Research & Strategic Reporting

To ground the foundation's programming in actual community needs, I conducted research into the state of game development in the Breda region — mapping the landscape of active studios, identifying the challenges developers face, and surfacing gaps the foundation could address. Findings were compiled into structured reports and presented directly to the board to inform strategic decisions about the foundation's direction and priorities.

This work sits at the intersection of my research background and my production role: it required qualitative investigation, stakeholder interviews, and the ability to translate findings into actionable organisational recommendations.

Operations & Coordination

Organisational Infrastructure

Day-to-day I manage the operational fabric of the foundation — board meeting preparation and agenda setting, document management including legal documentation, internal communication workflows, and maintaining the organisational calendar. A small foundation like Breda Game City runs on the quality of its systems, and building those systems from the ground up has been a significant part of the role.

External Outreach

I manage the foundation's relationships with external partners — local game studios, educational institutions, and industry organisations. This includes cold outreach to new potential partners, maintaining existing relationships, and representing the foundation at external events. The goal is to keep Breda Game City visible and relevant within the regional and national games ecosystem.

Cross-cultural Coordination

The Breda games community is international — students and professionals from across Europe and beyond. Coordinating events and programmes across different cultural expectations and communication styles has been a consistent part of the role, and one that maps directly onto the cross-cultural team coordination experience from my time at Gaming Minds Studios.
